# Profile Comparison Reflections

Each section below compares two user profiles — what the results looked like, what changed, and why it makes sense. Written so anyone can follow along, no coding experience needed.

---

## High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lofi

The pop profile wanted happy, upbeat music. The lofi profile wanted calm, quiet music — something you might study to. These are basically opposites, and the results reflect that. The pop user got Sunrise City at #1. The lofi user got Library Rain at #1. Both top picks matched the genre and the mood, which is exactly what you'd hope for.

One difference worth noticing: the lofi user's top 5 felt more consistent. All five songs were soft, slow, and mostly instrumental — a good study playlist. The pop user's top 5 felt a little off. The #2 song, Gym Hero, is technically labeled "pop," but it is an intense workout song, not a happy one. It showed up because the system gives a lot of points (2 full points) just for having the right genre tag. The mood — happy vs. intense — only adds 1 point. So a gym anthem beats out a happier song just because the genre label matches.

Think of it this way: you ask a friend for happy pop songs. They hand you Gym Hero because it is pop. Technically correct. Not what you meant.

## High-Energy Pop vs. Deep Intense Rock

Both profiles want high-energy music — pop around 0.85 energy, rock around 0.90. Those are close numbers. But the results look very different.

The pop user got two actual pop songs in the top 2. The rock user got one actual rock song at #1, and then four songs that have nothing to do with rock: a pop song, an electronic song, and two more pop songs. The reason is simple: there is only one rock song in the entire catalog. After Storm Runner takes the top spot, the system has nothing rock-related left to recommend. It just picks whatever high-energy songs are available, regardless of genre.

The same song, Gym Hero, shows up at #2 for both profiles. For the pop user, it is there because it is pop. For the rock user, it is there because it is intense and high-energy. Same song, completely different reason. The system does not know or care — it just adds up points.

## Chill Lofi vs. Deep Intense Rock

This is the biggest contrast. One profile wants quiet and calm. The other wants loud and intense. They have almost nothing in common.

The lofi results make sense end to end. All five songs are soft, slow, and peaceful. You could actually put those songs on and use them as intended. The rock results fall apart after the first song. Storm Runner is a great fit. But then the list fills in with pop and electronic songs that just happen to be energetic. A rock fan looking at that list would be confused.

The lesson here is that the system is only as good as the songs it has to choose from. The lofi user got good results because the catalog has three lofi songs and several other soft, acoustic songs nearby. The rock user got weak results because there is basically nothing rock in the catalog. But the system never says "we could not find enough matches." It just confidently shows five songs no matter what — even if four of them are wrong.

In a real music app, the rock user would just stop trusting the recommendations. They would not know why the suggestions feel off. They would just stop using it.
